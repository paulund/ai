# Reply Style Guide

## Purpose of a Reply

A good reply does one of three things:
1. Adds a fact, detail, or example the original post did not include
2. Offers a different angle or respectful counter-point with reasoning
3. Asks a genuine question that extends the conversation

A bad reply does one of three things:
1. Agrees without adding anything ("Great point!", "This!")
2. Promotes or links to your own content
3. Is too long — longer than the original post

---

## X Reply Conventions

**Length:** 1-2 sentences. Under 200 characters is ideal.

**Tone:** Casual, direct, as if you typed it immediately after reading.

**Format:**
- No bullet points
- No formal structure
- No "I agree with everything you said, but..."
- Fragment sentences are fine ("Exact same experience.")

**What works:**
- Sharing a specific experience that relates to the post
- Adding a concrete detail the original did not mention
- Asking a short, genuine follow-up question
- Disagreeing with one specific point and explaining why in a sentence

**What does not work:**
- Multi-sentence essays in a reply
- Starting with "I really resonated with this because..."
- Restating what the original post said before adding your point
- Any form of self-promotion or linking

**Examples:**

Original: "Most developers underestimate how much debugging eats into deep work time."

Good reply: "Especially true for anything asynchronous — adding proper logging up front saves hours later."

Bad reply: "I completely agree! This is such an important point. Context switching is really damaging. I wrote about this on my blog actually..."

---

Original: "AI is going to replace junior developers within 5 years."

Good reply: "I keep hearing this, but junior devs who learn to direct AI tools are outpacing mid-levels who don't. Different threat to what people think."

Bad reply: "That is a really interesting perspective. There are many factors to consider here. On one hand, AI is advancing rapidly. On the other hand, human creativity still has value..."

## LinkedIn Reply Conventions

**Length:** 2-4 sentences. Slightly more considered than X, but still concise.

**Tone:** Professional but human. Like a quick message from a colleague who read the post.

**Format:**
- Still no bullet points or structured formatting
- Can include a brief specific example or anecdote
- A genuine question at the end is appropriate
- Do not open with "Great post!" — lead with your actual point

**What works:**
- A short story or anecdote that adds to the conversation (1-2 sentences)
- A specific disagreement with reasoning
- Sharing what you have seen differently in your own work
- A question that invites the original poster to elaborate on something specific

**What does not work:**
- Opening with praise ("What a fantastic post, really resonated with me!")
- Vague agreement without substance
- Turning a reply into a mini-post
- Tagging others just to pull them into the thread

**Examples:**

Original: "The best investment you can make in your career is learning to communicate clearly."

Good reply: "Completely changed my approach to code reviews when I started writing comments for the 'why' rather than the 'what'. Takes longer to write but saves hours in context in the review thread."

Bad reply: "Absolutely! Communication is so important and often overlooked by technical people. It is one of the most valuable skills you can develop. Thank you for sharing this important message!"

---

Original: "Remote work is here to stay, regardless of what return-to-office mandates say."

Good reply: "The mandates often have more to do with commercial real estate commitments than productivity. The companies going hardest on RTO tend to be the ones with the biggest office leases."

Bad reply: "Such a great insight. Remote work has so many benefits for work-life balance and mental health. The data really does support this. I have seen it firsthand in my own career..."
