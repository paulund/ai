# LinkedIn Style Guide

## Format

- **150-300 words** — long enough to tell a story, short enough to hold attention
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences) — LinkedIn renders as a wall of text on mobile otherwise
- Line breaks between paragraphs for readability
- **Use dashes (`-`)** to list benefits, features, steps, or key points for scannability
- No code blocks — LinkedIn does not render them properly
- 3-5 hashtags at the very end, never embedded in body text
- **Copy-paste ready** — posts must be formatted so the user can select, copy, and paste directly into LinkedIn without editing

## Structure

1. **Hook** (1 line) — shows before "see more." Must earn the click. Lead with the problem you solved or the thing you observed, not an opinion-bait opener.
2. **Story/Context** (2-3 short paragraphs) — set the scene. What happened? What did you observe? Ground it in a specific, real experience.
3. **Insight** (1-2 paragraphs) — what did you learn? What would you tell someone facing this? This is the payoff.
4. **Question** (1 line, optional) — invite responses. Make it specific, not generic. "Has anyone else seen this with GCP?" beats "What do you think?"

## Tone

- **Professional but human** — write like you are talking to a senior colleague, not presenting at a board meeting
- **Measured and considered** — not shouty, not provocative. Have a point but do not force it
- **Storytelling over lecturing** — show, do not tell. "Last month I..." beats "Developers should..."
- **Honest about failures** — audiences respond to vulnerability more than perfection. But only real stories, never fabricated ones
- **Genuine enthusiasm** — "brilliant", "class", "worth it" are fine. "Groundbreaking", "incredible", "game-changing" are not
- **British English** throughout — spelling, phrasing, cultural references
- **First person** — this is your experience, own it
- **No corporate-speak** — ban: leverage, synergy, ecosystem, thought leader, stakeholders, circle back
- **No humble-brag** — "so grateful for this incredible opportunity" is transparent and boring
- **Sentence fragments are fine** when they earn their place. "Worth it." "Best decision we made."

## Hashtags

- 3-5 per post
- Always at the very end, after all content
- Never embedded in the body text (breaks reading flow)
- Mix broad (#SoftwareEngineering) and specific (#Laravel, #Contracting)

## What Works

- Work experience stories with real numbers or timelines
- Honest tech or industry assessments ("We chose X over Y, here is what happened")
- Lessons from real projects, anonymised where needed
- Career milestones and reflections
- Industry observations that affect the reader directly
- "Here is what I would do differently" retrospectives

## What Does Not Work

- Corporate-speak and jargon walls
- Humble-bragging ("So humbled to announce...")
- Emoji overuse (one or two is fine, emoji walls are not)
- Reposting articles without adding your own perspective
- Generic motivational content ("Hard work pays off!")
- Engagement bait ("Like if you agree, comment if you disagree")
- Copying an X post directly — different audience, different tone

## Examples

**Input idea:** "I made a major tech stack decision that was painful at first but paid off."

**Output post:**
```
The best technical decision I made last year looked like a mistake for three months.

We switched our API layer from REST to GraphQL. The first quarter was rough — schema design debates, resolver complexity, N+1 queries everywhere. The team questioned it constantly.

Then something clicked. The frontend team started shipping twice as fast. We killed 30 bespoke endpoints. Onboarding new features became predictable.

The lesson: good technical decisions often look bad before they look good. You need enough conviction in the why to get through the painful middle.

What technical decision have you made that took time to prove itself?

#SoftwareEngineering #TechLeadership #Engineering
```

**Input idea:** "AI coding tools are changing how development teams actually work."

**Output post:**
```
Six months ago I was sceptical about AI-assisted development.

Now I use it every day and I am training others to do the same.

What changed? I stopped treating it as autocomplete and started treating it as a junior developer who never gets tired. You still need to review everything. You still need to know what good code looks like. But the repetitive bits — boilerplate, test scaffolding, documentation — now take minutes instead of hours.

The engineers who push back hardest are usually the ones who have not tried it properly. The ones who have spent a week with it? They do not want to go back.

This is not replacing developers. It is making the good ones faster.

Has your team adopted AI-assisted workflows yet?

#AI #SoftwareEngineering #DeveloperProductivity
```
