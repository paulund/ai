# Copywriting Reference Guide

## The Goal of Marketing Copy

Copy has one job: move a specific person from awareness to action. Every word should serve that goal. Copy that is clever but unclear fails. Copy that is exhaustive but unfocused fails. Copy that is honest, specific, and benefits-led works.

---

## Language Rules

### Words to avoid

**Vague verbs:** streamline, optimise, empower, leverage, unlock, harness, revolutionise

**Filler adjectives:** seamless, powerful, robust, comprehensive, flexible, scalable, intuitive, intelligent

**Superlatives without proof:** world-class, industry-leading, best-in-class, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art

**Corporate jargon:** synergy, ecosystem, holistic approach, end-to-end, mission-critical, thought leadership

**Weak qualifiers:** "can help you", "may improve", "has the potential to", "could enable"

### What to use instead

| Instead of this | Write this |
| --- | --- |
| "Streamlines your workflow" | "Cuts deployment time from 45 minutes to 8" |
| "Powerful analytics" | "See which campaigns drove signups in real time" |
| "Intuitive interface" | "Set up in 10 minutes, no training required" |
| "Seamless integration" | "Connects to Slack, GitHub, and Jira out of the box" |
| "Empowers your team" | "Your team ships without waiting for IT approval" |

## Headline Formulas

**The outcome headline:** State what the customer gets.
- "Ship software 3x faster without the ops overhead"
- "Invoice clients in 30 seconds"
- "Run payroll in under 5 minutes"

**The problem headline:** Name the pain before offering the solution.
- "Tired of deployment scripts that break on Fridays?"
- "Your analytics data is lying to you"
- "Manual invoicing is costing you 4 hours a week"

**The who headline:** Speak directly to the target audience.
- "For teams that ship daily"
- "Built for freelancers who hate admin"
- "The tool solo founders actually use"

**The comparison headline:** Position against the old way.
- "Observability without the Datadog bill"
- "All the power of Salesforce, none of the complexity"
- "What spreadsheets were never meant to do"

## CTA Patterns

**Formula:** `[Action Verb] + [What They Get] + [Qualifier]`

| Page type | Primary CTA | Secondary CTA |
| --- | --- | --- |
| SaaS landing | Start Free Trial | See How It Works |
| Product demo | Book a Demo | Start Free |
| Pricing page | Get Started | Talk to Sales |
| Feature page | Try [Feature Name] Free | See All Features |
| Blog/content | Start Free Trial | Subscribe for Updates |

**Avoid:**
- Submit
- Click Here
- Learn More (too vague)
- Get Started (too generic — add what they are starting)

## Social Proof Patterns

List in order of persuasiveness:

1. **Named customer quotes with specific outcomes** — "We reduced churn by 22% in 3 months." — Jane Smith, Head of Product, Acme Corp
2. **Recognisable company logos** — no text needed
3. **Usage numbers** — "Used by 3,400 teams across 60 countries"
4. **Star ratings** — "4.8/5 on G2 (1,200+ reviews)"
5. **Press mentions** — "As featured in TechCrunch, The Verge"

Generic quotes without specifics ("Great product, highly recommend!") provide almost no lift. If the copy includes testimonials, they must include a specific outcome or result.

## Objection Handling

Common SaaS objections and how to address them in copy:

| Objection | How to address |
| --- | --- |
| "It looks complicated" | Show a 3-step process. Add "Set up in 10 minutes." |
| "I'm not sure it integrates with my stack" | List integrations explicitly. "Works with GitHub, Jira, Slack, and 40+ more." |
| "I don't want to commit without trying" | Lead with free trial or free tier. Remove risk language. |
| "Is my data safe?" | Security section or trust badges. SOC 2, GDPR, encryption details. |
| "What if I need help?" | Mention support: "24/7 live chat" or "dedicated onboarding call included." |
| "How does pricing scale?" | Transparent pricing or "no per-seat fees" if applicable. |

## Copy by Page Type

### Homepage

Goal: communicate who this is for and what it does in under 10 seconds.

Structure:
1. Headline: core value proposition
2. Subheadline: who it is for and what makes it different
3. Primary CTA
4. Social proof (logos or usage number)
5. Features/benefits (3-5, outcomes-led)
6. How it works (3 steps)
7. Testimonials (with specific outcomes)
8. Final CTA

### Pricing page

Goal: make the right tier obvious. Remove uncertainty about cost.

Must-haves:
- Feature comparison table (simple, not exhaustive)
- Highlighted recommended plan
- Monthly/annual toggle
- Annual discount shown clearly (17-20% is standard)
- FAQ addressing: "What happens when I exceed limits?", "Can I cancel anytime?", "Do you offer refunds?"
- Social proof near CTAs

### Feature page

Goal: explain what the feature does, why it matters, and who it is for.

Structure:
1. Headline: what the feature does
2. Subheadline: the problem it solves
3. 2-3 specific benefits with outcomes
4. Screenshot or demo
5. Use case: who uses it and how
6. CTA to try

## Common Mistakes

- Writing for the product instead of the customer ("We built this because...")
- Burying the CTA below the fold
- Using the same CTA text everywhere ("Sign Up" on every button)
- Writing paragraphs instead of short blocks — landing pages are scanned, not read
- Leading with company history or founding story on a landing page
- Using passive voice ("the app can be configured by users")
- Headline that describes the category rather than the outcome ("Project Management Software")
