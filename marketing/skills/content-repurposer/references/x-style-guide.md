# X (Twitter) Style Guide

## Philosophy

Posts should be **informational first**. Lead with clarity and facts. If you have a perspective or opinion, back it with reasoning or experience — do not just assert it. Avoid gimmicks and sensationalism. Write like you are sharing something useful with peers, not trying to go viral. No "hot take:" openers, no newspaper headline framing. Just say what you think naturally.

## Format

- **Flexible length** — long-form posts welcome; prioritise clarity over brevity
- Single posts for sharp takes; threads (3-5 posts) for deeper topics
- Line breaks for emphasis — do not write walls of text
- **Use dashes (`-`)** to list benefits, features, steps, or numbers — dashes copy-paste cleanly into X (not `•` dots)
- No hashtag spam — 0-1 per post, only if genuinely useful
- No emoji walls — one emoji maximum, and only if it adds tone
- **Copy-paste ready** — posts must be formatted so the user can select, copy, and paste directly into X without editing

## Tone

- Conversational, like talking to a colleague who works in a similar field
- Measured and considered, not shouty or provocative
- Clear and direct — prioritise information over cleverness
- Genuine enthusiasm is fine ("brilliant", "class", "worth it"). Hype is not ("groundbreaking", "incredible", "game-changing")
- British spelling and phrasing throughout
- First person when sharing experience; third person for factual reporting
- Natural rhythm — mix short and longer sentences. Sentence fragments are fine when earned ("Worth it." "Absolute class.")
- Straight delivery — no sarcasm (does not land well online), no fake storytelling

## Structure

**Single post:**
1. Lead with the key fact or insight
2. Add supporting detail or context
3. If you have a view on why it matters, explain that — do not just hint at it

**Thread:**
1. Post 1: State the topic clearly. Make people want the context.
2. Posts 2-N: One idea or fact per post. Build logically, not dramatically.
3. Final post: Takeaway, implication, or honest question — not clickbait

## What Works

- Technical or industry news with a clear explanation of why it matters
- "Thing I learned" observations grounded in real experience
- Practical insights from your own work with specific details
- Industry trends explained through your own lens and experience
- Honest assessment of trade-offs when you have lived through them
- Sharing numbers, timelines, or outcomes from real projects

## What Does Not Work

- "Hot take:" openers or newspaper headline framing
- Reposting news without explaining why it matters to you
- Contrarian for contrarian's sake — opinion without reasoning
- Generic motivational content
- Thread bait and cliffhangers ("You won't believe what happened next...")
- Tagging people for attention
- Gimmicks: heavy emoji, excessive formatting, fake urgency
- Manufactured urgency or fake storytelling
- Sarcasm

## Examples

**Input idea:** "I've been using AI coding tools for 6 months and it has genuinely changed how I work."

**Output post:**
```
6 months of daily AI-assisted development.

The shift is real. Not because it writes code for me — it does not. But because the boring parts (boilerplate, test scaffolding, basic CRUD) now take minutes instead of hours.

What changed: I stopped treating it as autocomplete and started treating it as a junior who never gets tired.

You still need to review everything. You still need to know what good looks like. But if you are spending time on repetitive scaffolding, you are leaving hours on the table.
```

**Input idea:** "We migrated from a monolith to microservices and it was painful."

**Output post:**
```
18 months into our monolith-to-microservices migration.

First 6 months were genuinely hard. Service boundaries were wrong, distributed tracing was a mess, and we introduced more latency than we solved.

But now:
- Deploys are independent
- Teams can move without stepping on each other
- Failures are contained

Worth it. Just budget for the pain and do not underestimate the observability work.
```

**Input idea:** "Most developers I know underestimate how much time they spend context-switching."

**Output post:**
```
Most devs underestimate how expensive context switching actually is.

It is not the switch itself — it is the 15-20 minutes to get back into flow after each one.

Three interruptions in a morning and you have lost an hour of deep work without noticing.

The fix is boring: block time, close Slack, and protect it like a meeting you cannot cancel.
```
