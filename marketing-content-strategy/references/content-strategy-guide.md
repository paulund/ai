# Content Strategy Reference Guide

## Why Most Content Strategies Fail

The three most common failure modes:

1. **No pillars** — publishing random topics that attract the wrong audience or no audience
2. **All awareness, no conversion** — lots of "how to" content, no comparison or pricing content that converts
3. **Volume over quality** — publishing 10 shallow posts instead of 3 deep, genuinely useful ones

A good content strategy solves all three: defined themes, balanced journey stages, depth over volume.

## Searchable vs Shareable: A Practical Breakdown

### Searchable content — building the foundation

These pieces are investments. They compound over time as search rankings build.

**Use-case pages**
Structure: `[Product or skill] + [specific job or outcome]`
Examples:
- "How to automate API testing with GitHub Actions"
- "Building a subscription billing system with Laravel"
- "Running remote performance reviews for engineering teams"

Target one specific use case per page. Do not write a general overview — write for someone who has a very specific problem.

**Hub-and-spoke clusters**
One pillar page covers the broad topic comprehensively. Supporting pages cover sub-topics in depth. Internal links connect them.

Example cluster for "API Security":
- Pillar: "API Security: The Complete Guide"
- Spokes: "OAuth 2.0 Implementation", "Rate Limiting Best Practices", "API Key Management", "Webhook Security"

**Comparison and alternatives pages**
Some of the highest-converting content. Target buyers actively evaluating options.
- `[Your product] vs [Competitor]` — be honest about trade-offs
- `Best [category] for [specific use case]` — position where you win
- `[Competitor] alternatives` — capture competitors' dissatisfied users

**Template and resource libraries**
Bookmark-worthy content that people return to. High shareability, strong SEO.
- Code snippets and starter templates
- Checklist PDFs
- Calculators and tools
- Glossaries for niche terms

### Shareable content — creating demand

These pieces attract audiences who do not yet know they need your product. They work best on social media and in newsletters.

**Original data**
Surveys, benchmarks, analysis of trends in your industry. "We analysed 500 developer productivity surveys" is a post. "Here are our thoughts on developer productivity" is not.

**Honest case studies**
Structure: problem → approach → specific result. Include numbers. Mention what went wrong.
Avoid: "We partnered with [company] to deliver transformational outcomes."
Prefer: "Acme Corp reduced deploy failures by 70% after switching from manual releases to automated pipelines. Here is exactly what they changed."

**Contrarian takes**
Disagree with conventional wisdom when you have evidence. Not for provocation — for insight.
Good: "Everyone recommends microservices for scale. Here is what the data actually shows for teams under 20 engineers."
Bad: "Hot take: microservices are overrated."

**Behind-the-scenes**
How decisions are made, what was tried and failed, why you built something the way you did. Audiences respond to process and honesty more than polish.

## Keyword Research in Practice

### Finding buyer-stage keywords

For a hypothetical SaaS product that handles API monitoring:

| Stage | Example keywords |
| --- | --- |
| Awareness | "what is API monitoring", "how to monitor API performance", "API latency explained" |
| Consideration | "best API monitoring tools", "Datadog vs Prometheus", "API monitoring alternatives" |
| Decision | "API monitoring pricing", "API monitoring free trial", "API monitoring reviews" |
| Implementation | "API monitoring setup tutorial", "API monitoring Kubernetes", "API alerting templates" |

The decision-stage keywords often have lower search volume but much higher commercial intent. Do not ignore them.

### Evaluating keyword difficulty

Before targeting a keyword, assess:
- Can you produce the best content on the internet for this search?
- Is there an established player with 10x your domain authority dominating it?
- Is the search intent a match for what you produce?

If all three answers are unfavourable, choose a more specific variation with lower competition.

## Content Calendar Principles

**Consistency beats frequency.** One genuinely useful post per week outperforms four shallow ones.

**Batch production.** Plan one month ahead, produce in batches. Writing 4 posts in one day is more efficient than writing 1 post per week.

**Promotion equals production.** For every hour spent writing, spend an equal hour distributing (email newsletter, social posts, community shares, link building outreach).

**Review monthly.** Check which pieces are getting traffic and engagement. Double down on what is working. Kill what is not.

## Ideation Sources

Use these to generate topic ideas that are grounded in actual customer needs:

1. **Support tickets and sales call transcripts** — what questions do people ask before buying?
2. **Reddit and community forums** — what problems are people discussing in your niche?
3. **Competitor content gaps** — what topics do competitors cover badly or not at all?
4. **Keyword research tools** — what are people actually searching for?
5. **Customer interviews** — what did customers try before finding you? What terms do they use?
6. **Internal expertise** — what does your team know deeply that others do not?

## Metrics by Content Type

Track different metrics for different content types:

| Content type | Primary metric | Secondary metric |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Searchable/SEO | Organic sessions | Keyword rankings |
| Thought leadership | Social shares and engagement | Newsletter subscribes |
| Case studies | Demo requests / trial signups | Time on page |
| Comparison pages | Conversion rate | Organic sessions |
| Tutorials/how-to | Return visitors | Shares |

Do not obsess over traffic if conversion is the goal. A low-traffic, high-conversion page beats a high-traffic page with zero intent.
